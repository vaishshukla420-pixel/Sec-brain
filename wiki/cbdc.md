**Central bank digital currencies are the state's answer to crypto — digital
cash as a direct central-bank liability — and the great irony is that the
US chose the opposite path: banning a Fed CBDC and deputizing private
stablecoins instead.**

The landscape: China's e-CNY is the scale leader (hundreds of millions of
wallets, still a small share of actual payments); the ECB's digital euro
grinds through preparation phases toward a possible launch late this
decade; ~100+ central banks run pilots; India pushes the e-rupee alongside
UPI. The design tensions are permanent: a retail CBDC competes with bank
deposits (disintermediation risk), and programmable state money raises
surveillance and control objections that make it politically radioactive
in the West.

The strategic story to watch is the fork in the road: the US banned a Fed
CBDC — by executive order in 2025, then by statute through 2030 (signed
into law 10-11 July 2026) — and passed the GENIUS Act instead, effectively
choosing regulated private [[stablecoins]] as the dollar's digital form
([[crypto-regulation]]). The EU went the other way: the European
Parliament backed the digital euro in June 2026, targeting pilots in 2027
and possible issuance ~2029, explicitly framed as reducing dependence on
US payment rails. Since ~99% of stablecoins are dollar-pegged, this
exports dollarization through crypto rails — and makes *stablecoin*
issuers, buying T-bills at scale, instruments of US monetary reach. CBDCs
elsewhere are partly a defense against exactly that. The privacy critique
CBDCs face is the mirror image of the [[privacy-tech]] fight: the same
ledger transparency, wielded by the state.
