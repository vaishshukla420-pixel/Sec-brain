**Centralized exchanges are crypto's banks in all but name — the on-ramps,
custodians, and casinos where most users actually touch the market — and
historically its single biggest point of failure.**

The business: spot and derivatives trading (perpetual futures dominate
crypto volume), custody, staking, listings. Binance leads globally;
Coinbase anchors the regulated US market (and custodies most of the
[[bitcoin-spot-etf]] assets); offshore venues run the leverage. The model's
pathologies are documented by its body count — [[mt-gox-collapse]] (2014),
[[ftx-collapse]] (2022) — and by Binance's own $4.3B US settlement (2023),
after which even the survivors accepted compliance as the price of scale.

Post-2022 reforms changed the sector's shape: proof-of-reserves became
standard, custody separated from trading in regulated markets (MiCA, US
rules — [[crypto-regulation]]), and institutions largely bypass exchanges
for qualified custodians. The standing structural tension: CeFi competes
with [[defi]] on convenience versus transparency — a CEX is fast and
familiar but a black box ([[self-custody]] rule applies); a DEX is
trust-minimized but self-serve. Every cycle, volume shifts a little
further on-chain.
