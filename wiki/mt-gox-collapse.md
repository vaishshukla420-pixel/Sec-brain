**In February 2014 Mt. Gox — then handling ~70% of all bitcoin trading —
froze withdrawals and filed for bankruptcy, revealing ~850,000 BTC lost to
years of undetected theft: crypto's first systemic custody failure.**

A Tokyo exchange that began as a trading-card site ended up as the world's
de facto bitcoin bank, run with amateur controls. Hackers had been draining
its hot wallets since at least 2011; the company papered over the hole
until it couldn't (about 200,000 BTC were later found in an old wallet,
leaving ~650,000 lost). Bitcoin's price fell ~50% and the first great
crypto winter followed.

Why it stays relevant: it set the template every later custodial disaster
followed — opaque books, commingled funds, insolvency hidden until a
withdrawal run ([[ftx-collapse]] is Mt. Gox with a Super Bowl ad budget).
It birthed the [[self-custody]] ethos, pushed Japan to become the first
country to license exchanges ([[crypto-regulation]] starts here), and its
decade-long bankruptcy — creditors finally repaid in 2024-25 in BTC worth
~50x the 2014 price — became a running lesson in how slowly legal systems
metabolize crypto. Rehypothecation of customer coins remains the #1 red
flag to check on any [[cefi-exchanges]] platform.
