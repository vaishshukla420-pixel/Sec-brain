**Bitcoin didn't appear from nowhere — it assembled thirty years of failed
digital-cash attempts, each of which had solved one piece but died on
centralization.**

The lineage: David Chaum's ecash/DigiCash (1980s-90s) invented
cryptographic private payments but required a company as issuer — it went
bankrupt in 1998. e-gold (1996) reached millions of users as digital gold
claims — and was shut down by US prosecutors because a central operator is
a legal single point of failure. Adam Back's Hashcash (1997) invented
proof-of-computation as anti-spam postage — the seed of [[proof-of-work]].
Wei Dai's b-money and Nick Szabo's bit gold (1998) sketched decentralized
money on computational cost but never solved consensus.

Satoshi's actual innovation in [[bitcoin-genesis]] was the *combination*:
Hashcash-style work + a peer-to-peer gossiped [[blockchain]] + longest-chain
consensus + issuance as the incentive to secure it. Every failed
predecessor died at its central chokepoint; Bitcoin's design removes the
chokepoint entirely. The cypherpunk mailing-list culture that produced all
of this remains crypto's ideological DNA — privacy, censorship resistance,
distrust of intermediaries ([[privacy-tech]]).
